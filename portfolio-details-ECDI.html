Early Childhood Development Index (ECDI 2030)

The ECDI 2030 was designed to provide a simple, globally applicable question set for measuring early childhood development. It is used in national health and education surveys, as well as UNICEF’s Multiple Indicator Cluster Surveys (MICS).

Goals

  • Develop a clear and easily translatable question set to assess early childhood development worldwide.
  • Ensure usability in national surveys and UNICEF’s Multiple Indicator Cluster Surveys (MICS).

Challenges

  • Coordinating with research teams across five countries, as well as managing stakeholders from governments, NGOs, and international organizations.
  • Training field interviewers with little-to-no qualitative research experience within a condensed 2–4 day period.

Methods and Tools

  • Coordinated 191 one-on-one cognitive interviews across five countries. Led the research teams in the United States, Jamaica, and India.
  • Identified and addressed four key sources of measurement error, including cultural misinterpretation and interviewer effects.
  • Performed statistical validation using exploratory and confirmatory factor analyses on existing MICS data.

Outcomes

Previous efforts to develop a cross-national, validated early childhood development (ECD) questionnaire led to overly complex instruments that increased respondent burden, resulting in lower response rates and item non-response. Initial cognitive testing and qualitative analysis revealed inconsistencies across countries, languages, and socio-economic groups. Factor analyses of survey data confirmed these issues, showing a lack of measurement invariance in the original instrument.

In response, I collaborated with UNICEF and subject matter experts to redesign the question set, addressing key sources of measurement error (e.g., linguistic interpretation and response bias). The final version demonstrated improved construct validity and cross-cultural consistency, significantly reducing respondent burden. The ECDI 2030 questionnaire was officially released and is now used worldwide to measure UN Sustainable Development Goal 4.2.1, helping shape early childhood policy.
